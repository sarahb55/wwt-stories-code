Bones’ existence suggests that a full Skeleton of the Milky Way can be found using proper “archaeological” tools. Beginning in 2013, the team starts working with undergrads to predict where other bones (notably spiral arms) of the Milky Way should lie on the Sky and in velocity space.


In early 2014, the best skeletal predictions are used to superimpose traces of the Milky Way spiral arms onto the Sky, within WorldWide Telescope. Click here now to see a snippet of the WWT Tour used by an NSF-sponsored summer undergraduate intern at the Harvard-Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics in the Summer of 2014, to look for “More Nessies” in infrared images of the Galactic Plane taken by Spitzer.

More Bones?
Using the results of their WWT-guided archaelogical “dig,” the team finds 10 likely new Bones. They ask the NSF to fund a project where these 10 Bones and many other (coarser) measures of galactic structure will be used to embark upon mapping the Skeleton of our Milky Way.
